From attacker types to attack process: understanding social engineering as a workflow

This page lists several kinds of attackers (script kiddies, identity thieves, social engineers, botnet operators, etc.). Instead of memorizing labels, it is useful to understand how certain attacks are carried out. Social engineering is not just phishing, it is a structured process that exploits human trust.

A simplified workflow of a social engineering attack:

  1. Reconnaissance. The attacker collects information about the target from public sources (social media, company websites, data leaks). Even small details (job role, recent projects, colleagues’ names) help to build credibility.
  2. Pretext creation and trust building. The attacker invents a believable scenario (e.g., IT support, bank employee, supplier) and initiates contact. The goal is to appear legitimate, not to rush.
  3. Exploitation. Once trust is established, the attacker persuades the victim to disclose information (passwords, codes) or perform an action (open an attachment, transfer money, approve login).
  4. Exit or persistence. After success, the attacker may disappear or try to maintain access for later use.

This perspective helps with the MCQs: social engineering is best described as influencing people to act against security policy, not just sending emails. Many techniques (phishing, baiting, pretexting, impersonation) fit inside this broader process.

From a defender’s point of view, breaking any phase stops the attack chain. For example, verifying unusual requests via an independent communication channel (calling back using an official number) can prevent successful impersonation.

Reference: NIST Computer Security Resource Center – Social Engineering definition

[CyBOK sec. 3.3]

Social engineering - to psychologically influence people into divulging secretive information or perform certain actions that may not be best for them.

Some popular techniques:
- Pretexting: providing victims with certain information (e.g. name of a bank, correct phone number etc.) to establish trust
- Water holing: tracking the victim's web-surfing habits and hijacking their trusted websites with malicious links
- Scareware: bombarding the victim with fake threats (e.g. a virus) and urging them to take action (e.g. give login information, delete certain files etc.)
- Baiting: the malicious payload is installed onto physical media (e.g USB), left in conspicuous areas with engaging labels, relies on curiosity and greed; can also be in an online form, such as "free" music downloads
- Quid Pro Quo: attacker offers valuable service or item in exchange of sensitive information (e.g. providing "free" IT services, then asking for login information)
- Phishing: deceiving people via electronic communications (e.g email, SMS, QR code, voice, etc.)

Source: Social engineering (security)
