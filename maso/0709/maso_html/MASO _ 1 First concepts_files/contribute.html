In my own understanding, none of these given options rightly describes what cyber-attack is all about; Let’s consider them one after the other:

  1. The target is not a private person using a single computer: Cyber-attacks such as phishing, malware and ransom ware can target either the individual users or large entities.
  2. The attack happens on behalf of a nation state: Many cyber-attacks are carried out by independent hackers or cyber-criminals even though some cyber-attacks are state-sponsored.
  3. The target suffers a substantial loss: I do not think the magnitude of the loss encountered during the attack is substantial enough to qualify it as a cyber-attack, this is because, even smaller attacks with little damage can still be regarded as a cyber-attack.
  4. The attacker is not acting alone but in a large coherent group: the number of attackers is not necessarily a defining factor because, cyber-attacks can be conducted by individuals as well as organized groups.

So what exactly is a Cyber Attack?
A cyber-attack is any intentional effort (by an individual or group of individuals) to steal, expose, alter, disable, or destroy data, applications, or other assets through unauthorized access to a network, computer system or digital device. Source: IBM

Teacher's note: This is a lengthy excerpt from the source. It is fine as an introduction the C-I-A triad, but as it also tries to cover the rest of this course, it is too long. Concentrating on the definitions would be better.

Confidentiality involves the efforts of an organization to make sure data is kept secret or private. To accomplish this, access to information must be controlled to prevent the unauthorized sharing of data—whether intentional or accidental. A key component of maintaining confidentiality is making sure that people without proper authorization are prevented from accessing assets important to your business. Conversely, an effective system also ensures that those who need to have access have the necessary privileges.

For example, those who work with an organization’s finances should be able to access the spreadsheets, bank accounts, and other information related to the flow of money. However, the vast majority of other employees—and perhaps even certain executives—may not be granted access. To ensure these policies are followed, stringent restrictions have to be in place to limit who can see what.

Integrity involves making sure your data is trustworthy and free from tampering. The integrity of your data is maintained only if the data is authentic, accurate, and reliable.

For example, if your company provides information about senior managers on your website, this information needs to have integrity. If it is inaccurate, those visiting the website for information may feel your organization is not trustworthy. Someone with a vested interest in damaging the reputation of your organization may try to hack your website and alter the descriptions, photographs, or titles of the executives to hurt their reputation or that of the company as a whole.

Availability means information should be consistently and readily accessible for authorized parties. Even if data is kept confidential and its integrity maintained, it is often useless unless it is available to those in the organization and the customers they serve. This means that systems, networks, and applications must be functioning as they should and when they should. Also, individuals with access to specific information must be able to consume it when they need to, and getting to the data should not take an inordinate amount of time.

If, for example, there is a power outage and there is no disaster recovery system in place to help users regain access to critical systems, availability will be compromised. Also, a natural disaster like a flood or even a severe snowstorm may prevent users from getting to the office, which can interrupt the availability of their workstations and other devices that provide business-critical information or applications. Availability can also be compromised through deliberate acts of sabotage, such as the use of denial-of-service (DoS) attacks or ransomware.

Cybersecurity :
  • Protection of internet-connected systems, including hardware, software and data from cyberattacks
  • Practice of protecting networks, programs, computer systems and their components from unauthorized digital access and attacks
  • Protection against unauthorized modification, unauthorised deletion, unauthorised access
  • Cyber security : set of techniques used to protect the integrity of networks
CIA triad :
  • Confidentiality : information and functions can be accessed only by authorized parties
  • Integrity : information and functions can be added, altered or removed only by authorized people and means
  • Availability : systems, functions and data must be available on demand according to agreed-upon parameters based on levels of service
Different attacks on CIA :
  • Confidentiality : cracking encrypted data, MITM (Man-in-the-Middle), data leakage/unauthorized copying of sensitive data, installing spyware/malware
  • Integrity : web penetration for malware insertion, malicously accessing servers, unauthorized database scans, remotely controling zombie systems
  • Availability : DoS/DDoS, ransomware - forced encryption of key data, flooding a server with too many requests

Cybersecurity is not only a technical field focused on protecting digital systems and data but also a socio-technical discipline, where human behavior, organizational policy, and technology interact. While traditional definitions emphasize confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA), modern cybersecurity also considers factors such as user awareness, legal frameworks, and ethical use of technology. Effective cybersecurity requires not only tools like firewalls, encryption, and authentication mechanisms, but also training, governance, and continuous risk assessment.

In a distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack, the target is overwhelmed by traffic coming from multiple sources, making it much harder to stop than a single-source attack. Blocking individual IP addresses is ineffective because the attack is distributed across many systems. A DDoS attack can be compared to a crowd of people blocking the entrance to a store, preventing legitimate customers from entering and causing disruption and financial loss. These attacks often target high-profile services such as banks or payment gateways, and are sometimes motivated by revenge, blackmail, or hacktivism. Understanding these basics helps identify why more advanced defensive strategies—such as traffic filtering, rate limiting, or using distributed content delivery networks—are essential to mitigate such threats.

For more information, see: Denial-of-service attack

    Cybersecurity: What is it?
Cybersecurity is the discipline in its own manner such as  networks, and systems against online threats.
